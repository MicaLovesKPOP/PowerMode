# Power Mode — Quick Guide

Power Mode has **5 profiles**.

**Recommended default:**  
**Optimized Performance** for a snappy gaming PC, or **Balanced Performance** for the best efficiency/noise balance.

## Important

The **3 Performance modes** should give practically the same gaming FPS.  
They all still allow the PC to boost properly when gaming or doing heavy work.

The real difference is mostly **desktop snappiness, power use, heat, fan noise,** and **thermal headroom**.

## Profiles

**Unrestrained Performance**  
Maximum snappiness. Keeps the CPU fully ready all the time.  
Usually overkill: more power use, heat, and fan noise, with little benefit.

**Optimized Performance**  
Best default for demanding users, or people who care about the system feeling extra snappy.  
Makes the PC feel just that little bit faster and more responsive, without going completely overboard.

**Balanced Performance**  
Normal Windows-style balanced behavior.  
Still performs properly in games, but lets the CPU relax more during light desktop use.  
**Best balance of speed, power use, heat, and noise.**

**Cool & Quiet**  
Reduces heat and fan noise by limiting how aggressively the PC boosts.  
Best for reducing power draw, reducing noise, or PCs that are thermally constrained.  
May reduce performance in heavy use, but can help if the PC is overheating or throttling.

**Extreme Energy Saver**  
For leaving the PC on while away.  
Good for downloads, file copies, cloud sync, updates, etc.  
Aggressively saves power and quiets the PC as much as possible, but this makes it very slow for actually using the PC.

## Basically

**Performance modes** = same gaming performance, different balance between snappiness and efficiency.  
**Cool & Quiet** = lower heat/noise, and useful if the PC is thermally struggling.  
**Extreme Energy Saver** = for leaving the PC on while you are not actively using it. Great for background tasks, bad for actually using the PC.
